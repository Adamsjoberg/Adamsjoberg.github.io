Wikipedia redesign

When looking at Wikipedia with the lens of accessibility, as it stands, there is much to be desired. The main menu is longer than most articles, the featured links on the main page are just big boxes of text, many features are hidden behind secret search syntax and some buttons have ambiguous names without context, making it hard to tell what they do. Wikipedia is made upon a classic hyperlink hierarchy, going from one article to the next seamlessly, but the result can be confusing to new users, as most of the site uses the same structure and templates. User pages look exactly the same as articles, for example. There are also important community guidelines and rules that are hidden to all but experienced users. In the testing of the site, one of my newly created articles got removed because of relevance guidelines that I had never heard of, and that was specific to that specific language version of Wikipedia.

Background

The main issue that Wikipedia faces is the decline in active contributors, also known as editors. They are the core of Wikipedia and without them articles are not written, translated or reviewed. Another issue is the lack of diversity, where 80% of the users are male. This leads to "female" topics not being covered to the same extent as typical "male" topics. In addition to this, the core users (editors) are reluctant to change and normal users (readers) are not keen on becoming editors.
